4 Simulation Setup

Our simulation setup involves simulating the weight of patients visiting their general physician for a checkup. For each patient, the sex (male, female) is always recorded. The data set is balanced in terms of sex, and we assume a mean weight for women of \(\mu_{f} = 65\) and for men a mean weight of \(\mu_{M} = 78\). The standard deviation is the same for both sexes: \(\sigma^2 = 15\).

# Simulate patient data
set.seed(123)

n <- 10000
sd_bmi <- 3
mu_bmi <- 25

sd_weight <- 15
mu_weight_f <- 65
mu_weight_m <- 78

weights <- tibble::tibble(
  idx = 1:n,
  sex = rep(x = c("F", "M"), each = n / 2),
  weight = c(
    rnorm(n / 2, mu_weight_f, sd_weight),
    rnorm(n / 2, mu_weight_m, sd_weight)
  ),
  bmi = rnorm(n, mu_bmi, sd_bmi)
)

We can see that the conditional distributions of weight given sex have different means.

ggplot(weights, aes(x = weight)) +
  geom_histogram(bins = 50) +
  facet_wrap(~ sex, nrow = 2, ncol = 1) +
  theme_bw() +
  labs(
    title = "Distribution of weight conditioning on sex",
    x = "Weight (kg)", y = "Count"
  )

We will setup up a simulation to illustrate the different mechanism patterns and how they affect the distributions of missing values and observed values. Suppose we have data on patients that are visiting a general physician. We consider two factors: age and cardiovascular disease. There are four groups of patients: young patients with and without cardiovascular disease (CVD) and old patients with and without CVD. These factors determine the mean of the population distribution of systolic blood pressure (SBP) in the underlying populations. Furthermore, we assume that the SBP is normally distributed and the variance is the same across all four groups: \(\sigma^2 = 15^2\).
